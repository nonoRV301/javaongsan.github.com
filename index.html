How to Control Mdi Form Menu Dynamically for Different Login User

Jan 19th, 2014 | Comments

Controlling winform for multi-user application can be easy achieve by limiting menu access based on login user. Most of the time, a role is required because many user have the same access rights. We can either add this manually or have everything store in an database.

```

foreach (ToolStripItem item in menuStrip1.Items)
{
    item.Enabled = RoleAccess.IsEnable(RoleId);
    item.Visible = RoleAccess.IsEnable(RoleId);
}

```

Building Your Own Software Product

Jan 18th, 2014 | Comments

This is not a guide, I do not have a profitable product yet, This is a collection for my own references.

If you been freelancing for a while, chances are you get the itch sometimes to create your own software product. For a freelance programmer, the best way to create recurring income is to create your own software product.

How do we go about creating an software product. First, find your niche. A niche is a group of people with the same behavior, in software sense, they use the same type of software to perform a common task.

When you have selected a niche, its time to do some research. Research your customers, look at what products they own, how much are they paying for. Go to forums, user groups, on facebook, twitter and linkedin. Find out what are their problems, pains, desires.

Generating Ideas for software.

Automated a time consuming task, build a companion to a task they need to automate e.g. a application that replace then need for a user to manually checking website related to their trade that do not provide filtering or email alerts.

Another great way to build software is to start out by solving your own problems. find other people who had the same problem. Look at what they are doing to solve it.

Look at what you are currently doing and what are your customer paying you to solve. Can some one else use it?

If someone have ask you about a certain kind of solution more than once, start taking notes.

Build your prototype, use agile methodology, start small learn and iterate. Then you’ll be able to build a product your customer truly love.

For the next 30 days, find at least one problem per day. Record it.

Creating a Reliable Lead Generation Channel for Your Freelancing Business

Jan 14th, 2014 | Comments

When I started freelancing I did not have a constant flow of clients. I search job boards, forums and classified ads, there were not many jobs and it was hard to convert those prospects into clients. I didn’t know what to do.

Since then I have gather a few methods from various freelancer and marketers I came across that have work well.

Define The Types of Client

For me a good client will be someone for pays $5000 or more for a single project. Loosely translate to $500 per man-day charge. Anything less are just not worth the time and effort even if I have no income. Recurring revenue is another thing that I would focus on. Clients I take on should be coming back to me for more work.

Lead Source

Leads can be generated through: 1. On-line classified ads. This are slow, and may generate 1 or 2 leads per month, but maintenances is easy. Most of the ads run for a month, then you go back and turn it on again. Use Google calender to set reminders, keep the url in the calender, so when an reminder is send, all you need to do is click the url. 2. Blog It takes a lot effort to get ranked in the search engine. Some SEO knowledge required here 3. PPC Paid Ads Using Adwords and facebook ads can generate the fastest and most relevant leads. 4. Linkedin People are always looking for someone good in certain skill set. Setting up a good profile can help generate a fair amount of traffic

Building the list

Now you have gather all the source, you need channel them to a place where you can keep build relationships. Use an opt-in list builder like mailchimp. Set up a landing page to catch all that traffic. Set up an series of emails.

Other more manual generation

Responding to wanted ads, forum query and user groups and posting links back to your blog post which eventually end up on your landing page.

Freelancing

Jan 13th, 2014 | Comments

More programmers are try their hands at freelancing full-time. They are driven by the dream to be independent and living without a 9-to-5 job constraints. Does a full-time freelancer get to work he you want, take vacations when you want, for as long as he wants. For me its not, I start freelancing 2 years ago, this are somethings I learn along the way. If you’re looking to start freelancing and not sure how you can go about it, then read on.

Motivation

Decide why you want to freelance. More time for family, more time for health, less commuting, no office politics, no more long boring meetings or the comfort of working from your own home. Knowing your motivation will help will go through tough times and taking full time employment again.

Finding work

The one most ask question I get when I introduce my self as a freelancer is “How to get work?”. Well, there are a few ways to find work: 1. Referral from a friends, family member, Ex-Employees, Ex-Colleagues or just about anyone you knows. 2. Your Website or Blog 3. From online job boards or on a freelance website like Elance, ODesk, or Freelancer.com 4. From online classified ads. All of the above requires constantly looking, goggling, searching and talking to people. It will probably take up more time then development before you get your first 3 clients. I have the most success getting referrals from ex-colleagues and ex-employers.

Whats my rate?

The next most important thing is how much to charge? Working out how much to charge clients is very difficult. I would recommend you work out you hourly rate. Try this formula if you are having trouble determine you hourly rate. Take your highest monthly pay, divide it by the number of working days, divide the result by 8, then increase it by 20%. You should get a reasonable hourly rate. Remember if you have low rate, you have a hard time trying to raise it later on.

To sum it up, as a freelancer, your next paycheck is never guaranteed. You are always searching for your next project while you chase the payment of the previous one.
